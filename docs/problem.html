What’s happening

Youth mental health has worsened over the last decade. At the same time, teens spend more time inside digital environments shaped by AI-driven feeds and recommendations. This project focuses on how that combo can increase risk — and why schools and policymakers need better information to respond.

Core idea: When screen time rises and online content gets more “sticky,” well-being can drop — and the students who may need support most aren’t always the ones who feel connected to trusted help.

How AI-driven platforms intensify risk

1) Engagement beats well-being

  • Algorithms prioritize content that keeps people scrolling (not what’s healthiest).
  • Negative, emotional, or extreme content can get rewarded with attention.
  • Recommendation systems can lead users from “mild” content to more intense material.

2) Social comparison is built in

  • Curated feeds can amplify body-image pressure and “everyone is happier than me” thinking.
  • Likes/metrics can turn identity and popularity into a constant scoreboard.
  • Sleep loss from late-night use can make stress and mood worse the next day.

Who is most affected

Digital harms don’t hit everyone evenly. Some groups face extra exposure to harassment, discrimination, or targeted content — and may have fewer offline supports available.

  • Girls often face stronger appearance pressure and social comparison.
  • LGBTQ+ youth may face higher harassment risk and fewer safe offline spaces.
  • Students of color can experience bias, hate content, and unequal access to support.

Why this is a policy problem (not just “personal choice”)

This isn’t only about individual self-control. The environment is engineered: AI systems shape what youth see, how long they stay, and what content gets repeated. That means the response can’t rely on “just log off” — it needs better guardrails and better support systems.

The biggest obstacle: data gaps

Schools and public agencies don’t have enough timely, usable information to track digital well-being risks or evaluate what actually helps. Platforms hold detailed behavioral data, but it’s not shared in consistent, policy-ready ways.

  • Public surveys capture trends, but often with limited detail and slow turnaround.
  • Platforms have the “real-time” picture, but the public can’t easily audit or compare it.
  • Without better data, it’s hard to target resources, measure impact, or build smart regulation.

This is why the next sections matter: the Evidence & Data page shows what national survey data can already tell us, and the Policy Solution page explains how a stronger data infrastructure can support youth protection and school-based help.

Stakeholders

  • Adolescents (health, identity, safety)
  • Parents & caregivers (guidance, monitoring, support)
  • Schools (prevention, counseling capacity, trusted access points)
  • Mental health professionals (care pathways, referral networks)
  • Technology companies (design choices, transparency, accountability)
  • Policymakers (public health response, standards, oversight)
