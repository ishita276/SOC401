Policy options

Addressing youth mental health risks linked to AI-driven digital platforms requires policy responses that go beyond individual behavior change. Policymakers face several potential approaches, each with distinct strengths and limitations.

Option 1: Platform risk assessments

Platform risk assessment requirements would mandate that technology companies regularly evaluate and disclose how their products may affect youth mental health. These assessments are intended to increase transparency and encourage companies to identify harmful design features.

  • Can expose internal knowledge about how algorithms amplify harmful content or encourage excessive engagement.
  • Creates a formal record that regulators and researchers can review.
  • Signals that youth mental health is a public responsibility, not just a private concern.

However, this approach relies heavily on self-reporting and enforcement. Without independent data or standardized metrics, risk assessments may vary widely in quality and offer limited insight into real-world impact. On their own, they provide information but not necessarily timely action.

Option 2: Youth-centered design requirements

Youth-centered design regulations focus on modifying platform features to reduce harm. This can include high-privacy defaults for minors, restrictions on addictive design elements, and limits on targeted recommendations.

  • Directly addresses features known to increase excessive use and emotional stress.
  • Shifts responsibility away from individual self-control and toward platform design.
  • Offers clear, enforceable rules that can reduce some forms of harm.

While important, design requirements face practical limits. Platforms evolve quickly, and static rules may struggle to keep pace with new features or emerging risks. Without ongoing data, it is difficult to know which design changes are most effective or whether unintended consequences arise.

Option 3: Federal Youth Digital Well-Being Data Initiative (recommended)

The Federal Youth Digital Well-Being Data Initiative addresses a core weakness shared by other approaches: the lack of timely, standardized information about how digital environments affect young people.

This initiative would establish a federally coordinated framework for collecting, analyzing, and sharing data on youth screen use, mental well-being, and access to school-based supports - while protecting privacy and student autonomy.

  • Creates consistent indicators that allow comparison across time, platforms, and populations.
  • Enables early identification of emerging risks rather than reacting only after harm becomes widespread.
  • Supports schools and public agencies with evidence to target resources where they are most needed.
  • Improves transparency without requiring disclosure of individual-level data.

Importantly, this approach does not replace regulation or design requirements. Instead, it strengthens them by providing the evidence base needed to evaluate effectiveness, refine policy, and hold systems accountable over time.

Why the data initiative is the strongest foundation

The evidence presented in the Evidence & Data section shows that youth digital risk is widespread but unevenly distributed. Students who report the greatest distress are often the least likely to feel supported by existing systems.

Without better data, policymakers are left responding to headlines rather than trends. A federal data initiative shifts the response from reactive to preventative by making youth digital well-being visible and measurable.

  • It complements platform oversight rather than duplicating it.
  • It supports school-based mental health systems with actionable insight.
  • It allows future regulation to be targeted, proportional, and evidence-driven.

In short, effective youth digital policy requires more than rules alone. It requires a shared understanding of risk, grounded in data that serves the public interest.
