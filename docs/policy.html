Policy Options

Policymakers have several possible approaches for addressing the growing impact of AI-driven digital platforms on youth mental health. Each option offers potential benefits, but they differ in scope, feasibility, and effectiveness.

1. Platform Risk Assessments

This approach would require technology companies to evaluate and disclose the potential mental health risks associated with their products and algorithms.

  • Encourages platforms to identify and acknowledge potential harms.
  • Improves transparency around design choices and content amplification.
  • Relies heavily on self-reporting and enforcement capacity.

2. Youth-Centered Design Requirements

Youth-centered design rules would mandate safer default settings and restrict features known to increase excessive engagement among adolescents.

  • Stronger privacy defaults for minors.
  • Limits on features that promote compulsive use.
  • Can reduce harm, but may struggle to adapt to rapidly changing platforms.

3. Federal Youth Digital Well-Being Data Initiative (Recommended)

The recommended approach focuses on building a stronger public data infrastructure to monitor youth digital well-being and guide evidence-based policy responses.

  • Establishes standardized indicators for youth screen use, mental health, and access to school-based supports.
  • Enables privacy-protective, aggregated data sharing between platforms, researchers, and public agencies.
  • Supports real-time dashboards and early warning systems to identify emerging risks.

Why this approach is preferred

As the evidence on the Evidence & Data page shows, youth mental health risks linked to digital environments are widespread, unevenly distributed, and difficult to detect early.

Without timely and comparable data, policymakers are forced to respond reactively—often after harm has already occurred. A federal data initiative addresses this gap directly by making youth digital well-being visible, measurable, and actionable.

  • It complements platform regulation rather than replacing it.
  • It helps schools anticipate need instead of responding only in crisis.
  • It creates accountability without relying solely on individual behavior change.

In short, better data is not a substitute for regulation—it is the foundation that makes effective, targeted policy possible.
