Interactive Data Dashboard

This page brings together national survey data to explore how adolescent screen time, mental well-being, and perceptions of school-based mental health supports are connected. The visualizations below combine findings from the Monitoring the Future (MTF) study and the Youth Risk Behavior Survey (YRBS).

Rather than focusing on screen time alone, this dashboard highlights how digital habits intersect with student happiness and confidence in school mental health resources.

How to use this dashboard

  • Interactive charts allow you to hover over data points to see exact percentages.
  • Legends can be clicked to hide or show specific outcomes.
  • All percentages represent within-group comparisons, not totals across all students.

Digital Well-Being Profile by Screen Time

The radar chart summarizes how well-being and support perceptions cluster together within each screen-time group. Each shape represents a different level of screen use.

  • Larger shapes indicate higher shares of students reporting positive outcomes.
  • Smaller shapes suggest weaker well-being or lower confidence in supports.
  • Differences between groups highlight how screen time may compound emotional and institutional support gaps.

What the MTF data shows

Across the MTF visualizations, a consistent pattern emerges. Students with lower daily screen time are more likely to report being very happy and to view school-based mental health supports as helpful.

  • Higher screen use is associated with lower reported happiness.
  • Students in the 7+ hour group are less likely to see counseling and mental health resources as effective.
  • These trends suggest that heavy digital engagement may coincide with weaker emotional well-being and lower institutional trust.

Screen Time by Gender (YRBS 2021)

While the MTF data shows how outcomes differ by screen time, YRBS data highlights how common high screen use is among U.S. high school students.

The chart below shows the share of students reporting 3 or more hours of daily screen time outside of schoolwork, broken down by gender.

What the YRBS data adds

While the MTF dashboard shows how outcomes differ by screen time, the YRBS data highlights how widespread high screen use is among U.S. high school students.

  • A large share of students report spending 3 or more hours per day on screens outside of schoolwork.
  • High screen time is common across genders, not limited to a small subgroup.
  • This means the risks identified in the MTF data affect millions of students nationwide.

Putting the findings together

Viewed together, the MTF and YRBS data tell a consistent story. High screen time is not only common among U.S. adolescents, but it is also linked to lower emotional well-being and weaker connections to school-based mental health supports.

  • YRBS shows prevalence: high screen time is a normal part of daily life for many students.
  • MTF shows consequences: students with heavier screen use are less likely to report being very happy.
  • Students with high screen time are also less likely to view counseling and mental health resources as helpful.

This combination is especially concerning. The students who may benefit the most from school-based mental health supports appear to be the least connected to them.

From screen time to support systems

Taken together, the MTF and YRBS findings raise a critical follow-up question: Do school-based mental health supports reach students equitably - and do students perceive them as helpful across demographic groups?

Exploring differences across students

The interactive dashboard below allows users to explore how perceptions of school-based mental health supports vary across different student groups. Filters can be used to compare patterns by race, gender, happiness, and future outlook.

Rather than showing one average experience, this dashboard highlights how mental health support is perceived differently depending on who students are and how they feel about their lives and futures.

Interactive Mental Health Support Dashboard

  • Use the dropdown filters to explore differences by race, gender, happiness level, and future outlook.
  • Charts update automatically based on your selections.
  • All charts display the distribution of responses within each group.

Key patterns from the interactive dashboard

  • Race: Perceptions of counseling and mental health resources vary by race, with some groups more likely to report that supports were not offered or not helpful.
  • Gender: Differences by gender suggest that access to and trust in school-based mental health supports is not evenly distributed.
  • Happiness: Students who report lower happiness are less likely to view counseling and mental health resources as helpful, even though they may need support the most.
  • Future outlook: Students who feel pessimistic about their future are more likely to report weak connections to school mental health supports.

Why these filters matter

The slicers at the top of the dashboard are not just design features, they are central to understanding risk and inequity.

  • Filtering by happiness shows that students with poorer emotional well-being are often the least likely to find school supports helpful.
  • Filtering by future outlook highlights a group of students who may feel disconnected not only from school, but from long-term support systems.
  • Looking across race and gender reveals gaps that may reflect differences in access, cultural relevance, or trust in school services.

These patterns suggest that need alone does not guarantee access - and that existing supports may not be reaching students most at risk.

Why this matters for policy

The interactive dashboard reinforces the same conclusion seen in the MTF and YRBS analyses: mental health risk is not evenly distributed, and current school-based supports do not consistently align with student need.

  • Efforts to improve adolescent mental health cannot focus only on individual behavior or screen limits.
  • Schools must also invest in making mental health supports visible, accessible, and trusted.
  • This includes normalizing help-seeking, improving counselor access, and ensuring services reach students most affected by heavy digital use.

Together, the YRBS and MTF findings support the core argument of this project: addressing adolescent mental health requires both reducing harmful digital overload and strengthening school-based mental health infrastructure.
