Interactive Data Dashboard

This page brings together national survey data to explore how adolescent screen time, mental well-being, and perceptions of school-based mental health supports are connected. The visualizations below combine findings from the Monitoring the Future (MTF) study and the Youth Risk Behavior Survey (YRBS).

Rather than focusing on screen time alone, this dashboard highlights how digital habits intersect with student happiness and confidence in school mental health resources.

How to use this dashboard

  • Interactive charts allow you to hover over data points to see exact percentages.
  • Legends can be clicked to hide or show specific outcomes.
  • All percentages represent within-group comparisons, not totals across all students.

Positive Outcomes by Screen Time

This interactive chart shows how three indicators of student well-being vary across levels of weekday screen time: being very happy, finding school counseling helpful, and finding school mental health resources helpful.

Screen time is grouped into three categories: less than 3 hours, 3–6 hours, and 7+ hours per weekday. Hover over the chart to see exact values and use the legend to compare outcomes across groups.

Digital Well-Being Profile by Screen Time

The radar chart summarizes how well-being and support perceptions cluster together within each screen-time group. Each shape represents a different level of screen use.

  • Larger shapes indicate higher shares of students reporting positive outcomes.
  • Smaller shapes suggest weaker well-being or lower confidence in supports.
  • Differences between groups highlight how screen time may compound emotional and institutional support gaps.

What the MTF data shows

Across the MTF visualizations, a consistent pattern emerges. Students with lower daily screen time are more likely to report being very happy and to view school-based mental health supports as helpful.

  • Higher screen use is associated with lower reported happiness.
  • Students in the 7+ hour group are less likely to see counseling and mental health resources as effective.
  • These trends suggest that heavy digital engagement may coincide with weaker emotional well-being and lower institutional trust.

What this dashboard shows

These charts draw on national data from the Monitoring the Future (MTF) survey of 12th graders. Students are grouped by weekday screen time (<3 hours, 3–6 hours, and 7+ hours of video or TV use), and outcomes focus on well-being and perceptions of school-based mental health supports.

The dashboard highlights three key outcomes:

  • Very happy – students who describe themselves as very happy overall.
  • Counseling helpful – students who say school counseling is quite or extremely helpful.
  • Mental health resources helpful – students who say school mental health resources are quite or extremely helpful.

Looking across the visualizations, a consistent pattern emerges. Students with lower daily screen time are more likely to report being very happy and to view school-based supports as helpful. As screen time rises into the 7+ hour range, reported happiness declines, and fewer students express strong confidence in counseling and mental health resources.

  • Lower screen time is associated with higher emotional well-being.
  • Students with heavier screen use are less likely to see school mental health supports as effective.
  • These gaps suggest that students most engaged online may also be least connected to trusted support systems.

When viewed alongside YRBS data—which shows that high screen time is common among U.S. high school students— these findings reinforce the central argument of this project. Schools must not only address heavy digital use, but also strengthen visible, accessible, and trusted mental health supports, especially for students who appear most at risk.

From screen time to support systems

Taken together, the MTF and YRBS findings raise a critical follow-up question: Do school-based mental health supports reach students equitably—and do students perceive them as helpful across demographic groups?
