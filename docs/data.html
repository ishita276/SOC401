Interactive Data Dashboard

This dashboard brings together national data from the Monitoring the Future (MTF) study to explore how high school students' screen time relates to happiness and views of school mental health support.

1. Positive Outcomes by Screen Time (Interactive)

How to use: Hover over the points and lines to see the exact percentage of students who are very happy, who find school counseling helpful, and who find school mental health resources helpful at each level of weekday screen time. Click items in the legend to hide or show each outcome.

2. Digital Well-Being Profile by Screen Time (Interactive Radar)

How to use: Each colored shape represents one screen-time group (<3 hours, 3–6 hours, 7+ hours). The farther a point is from the center, the higher the percentage of students in that group who report that outcome. Hover to see the exact values; toggle groups on and off using the legend.

What this dashboard shows

These charts use MTF survey data on 12th graders. Screen-time is grouped into three categories: less than 3 hours, 3–6 hours, and 7+ hours of video/TV on devices on a typical weekday. The outcomes are:

  • Very happy – students who describe themselves as very happy overall.
  • Counseling helpful – students who say school counseling is quite or extremely helpful.
  • Mental health resources helpful – students who say school mental health resources are quite or extremely helpful.

Looking across the dashboard, students with lower daily screen time are more likely to report being very happy and to see school-based supports as helpful. As screen time rises into the 7+ hour range, the share of “very happy” students drops, and fewer students feel strongly positive about school counseling and mental health resources.

Together with the YRBS findings (which show how common high screen time is among U.S. high school students), this dashboard supports the argument in my op-ed and policy brief: schools need to both address heavy digital use and strengthen visible, trusted mental health supports, especially for the students who are online the most.
